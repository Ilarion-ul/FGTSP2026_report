# The NSC KIPT Neutron Source (ADS): Concept, Status, and Neutron Yield

## Slide 1 — The NSC KIPT Neutron Source: Concept and Purpose

- **Facility type:** A hybrid nuclear–accelerator system known as an **Accelerator Driven System (ADS)**  
  [Karnaukhov et al., 2024; Vodin et al., 2013].

- **Strategic mission:** Creation of a modern experimental base in Ukraine for **neutron research** in fundamental science and **nuclear medicine**  
  [NSC KIPT, 2004; Pei et al., 2013].

- **Key capabilities:**
  - Production of medical radioisotopes for cancer therapy, e.g. **$^{99}$Mo, $^{188}$Re, $^{103}$Pd**  
    [Talamo & Gohar, 2008; NSC KIPT, 2004].
  - Development of **Boron Neutron Capture Therapy (BNCT)**  
    [NSC KIPT, 2004].
  - Advanced research in **reactor physics** and **radiation materials science**  
    [Vodin et al., 2013; Karnaukhov et al., 2024].
  - Training platform for **nuclear industry specialists**  
    [International Atomic Energy Agency, 2021].


## Slide 2 — Context, History, and Current Status

- **International collaboration:** Developed via partnership between **NSC KIPT (Ukraine)** and **Argonne National Laboratory (USA)**  
  [Gohar et al., 2022; Zelinsky et al., 2023].

- **Technical partners:** The driver linear accelerator (**LINAC**) was designed and manufactured by the **Institute of High Energy Physics (China)**  
  [Aizatskiy et al., 2013; Chi et al., 2011].

- **Milestones:**
  - **2010–2021:** Design, construction, and equipment mounting  
    [Karnaukhov et al., 2024].
  - **Physical start-up:** Successfully performed in **2021**  
    [Karnaukhov et al., 2024].

- **State after Feb 24, 2022:** Despite the full-scale invasion, NSC KIPT continues commissioning and analyzing physical start-up data to ensure nuclear safety and prepare for pilot operation  
  [Karnaukhov et al., 2024; Zelinsky et al., 2023].


## Slide 3 — Physical Principles: Primary Neutron Production

- **The driver:** A high-power LINAC delivers a beam of **100 MeV electrons** with an average power of **100 kW**  
  [Bezditko et al., 2017; Pei et al., 2013].

- **Target interaction chain:**
  - **Bremsstrahlung:** Electrons decelerate in the target, emitting high-energy **$\gamma$-quanta**  
    [Karnaukhov et al., 2024; Gann et al., 2021].
  - **Photonuclear reactions:** The produced photons induce **$(\gamma,n)$** reactions in heavy nuclei, generating **primary neutrons**  
    [Gann et al., 2021; International Atomic Energy Agency, 2021].

- **Target material options:**
  - **Tungsten (W):** used during start-up for high thermal stability  
    [Karnaukhov et al., 2023; Borts et al., 2024].
  - **Uranium (U–Mo):** provides ~2× higher neutron yield due to additional **photo-fission** reactions  
    [Vodin et al., 2013; Gann et al., 2024].


## Slide 4 — Physical Principles: Subcritical Multiplication

- **Subcritical core:** Loaded with **Low Enriched Uranium (LEU)** fuel with enrichment **< 20%**  
  [Gohar et al., 2022; Vodin et al., 2013].

- **Neutron amplification:** The subcritical assembly (SCA) acts as an “amplifier,” increasing neutron intensity by
  \[
  M = (1 - k_{\mathrm{eff}})^{-1}
  \]
  [Zelinsky et al., 2023].

- **Inherent safety:**
  - Effective multiplication factor is strictly **$k_{\mathrm{eff}} \le 0.98$**  
    [NSC KIPT, 2004; Vodin et al., 2013].
  - A self-sustaining chain reaction is physically impossible; the process stops when the electron beam is switched off  
    [NSC KIPT, 2004; International Atomic Energy Agency, 2021].

- **Reflector and moderator:**
  - Reflectors: **Beryllium** and **Graphite** (to improve neutron economy)  
  - Moderator: **demineralized water**  
  [Zelinsky et al., 2023; Karnaukhov et al., 2024].


## Comparative Assessment of Neutron Yield

### Integral neutron output (at 100 kW beam power)

| Target option | Integral neutron output |
|---|---|
| **Uranium target (U–Mo)** | $(3.01–3.28)\times 10^{14}\ \mathrm{n/s}$ |
| **Tungsten target (W–Ta)** | $(1.88–1.91)\times 10^{14}\ \mathrm{n/s}$ |

Sources: [Vodin et al., 2013; NSC KIPT, 2004].

### Generation efficiency (per incident electron at 100 MeV)

| Target option | Neutron yield per electron | Relative |
|---|---:|---:|
| **Uranium (U–Mo)** | $\approx 0.05\ \mathrm{n/e}$ (~5%) | 1.0× |
| **Tungsten (W–Ta)** | $\approx 0.025\ \mathrm{n/e}$ (~2.5%) | ~0.5× |

Sources: [Vodin et al., 2013; Argonne National Laboratory, 2008].

### Physical factors for increased yield in uranium

- Additional neutrons from photo-fission of **$^{238}$U** induced by hard $\gamma$-quanta and fast neutrons  
  [Vodin et al., 2013; Zelinsky et al., 2023].
- Uranium has comparatively negligible neutron absorption versus tungsten’s higher absorption cross-section  
  [Vodin et al., 2013].

### System impact (flux in the subcritical assembly)

- Center-core neutron flux:
  - **Uranium target:** $1.95\times 10^{13}\ \mathrm{n/cm^2\cdot s}$
  - **Tungsten target:** $1.14\times 10^{13}\ \mathrm{n/cm^2\cdot s}$  
  [Vodin et al., 2013; Zelinsky et al., 2023].

- Overall flux reduction with tungsten:
  - Approximately **4× lower** due to combined effects of lower primary yield and deeper required subcriticality  
    (typical example: $k_{\mathrm{eff}} = 0.96$ vs $0.98$)  
  [Vodin et al., 2013].
